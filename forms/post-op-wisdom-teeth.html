Post-operative Instructions

Wisdom Tooth Removal — what to expect and how to care for yourself

What to Expect

Swelling, bruising, and discomfort are normal and expected after wisdom tooth removal. Swelling typically peaks at 48–72 hours and then gradually improves over the following week. Bruising may appear on the cheeks, jaw, or neck — this is also normal and will fade.

Most patients feel significantly better by day 5–7, though everyone recovers at a different rate. You may experience mild residual soreness for up to 2 weeks. If you have concerns about your recovery, please don't hesitate to contact us.

Bleeding
  • Some oozing is normal for the first few hours after surgery.
  • Bite firmly on the gauze provided for 30–45 minutes. If the gauze becomes saturated, replace it with fresh gauze and continue biting firmly.
  • Do not rinse vigorously, spit forcefully, or use a straw for the first 24 hours — suction and pressure dislodge the blood clot essential to healing.
  • If bleeding is persistent: bite firmly on a damp tea bag for 20–30 minutes. The tannins in tea help promote clotting.
⚠ Seek urgent care if: you experience heavy, uncontrolled bleeding; large clots are forming and being dislodged; or you feel faint or weak from blood loss. Go to your nearest emergency department or call 000.
Swelling & Pain Management
  • Apply ice packs (wrapped in a cloth) to the outside of your face for 20 minutes on, 20 minutes off, for the first 24 hours. This reduces swelling.
  • After 48 hours, switch to warm packs to help resolve swelling more quickly.
  • Take pain relief before the local anaesthetic wears off — do not wait until you are in significant pain. It is much harder to control pain once it sets in.
  • Recommended: ibuprofen 400mg + paracetamol 500mg alternated every 4 hours (unless contraindicated by your medical history or surgeon's advice). Do not exceed recommended doses.
  • If you have been prescribed stronger pain relief, take it as directed and do not drive or operate machinery while taking it.
Diet
Your diet in the days after surgery can make a significant difference to your comfort and healing.
  • First 3–5 days: soft, cool foods — yoghurt, smoothies, scrambled eggs, mashed potato, cool soup, ice cream, soft pasta. Avoid anything requiring biting or chewing near the surgical sites.
  • Progress to normal soft foods as your comfort allows over the following week.
  • Stay well hydrated — drink plenty of water throughout the day.
  • Avoid hard, crunchy, or chewy foods (bread crusts, chips, nuts, tough meat) until fully healed.
  • Avoid hot food and drinks for the first 24 hours — heat increases bleeding.
  • Avoid alcohol for 48 hours — it thins the blood, promotes bleeding, and interferes with pain relief.
  • Avoid spicy foods until the surgical sites are fully healed.
Oral Hygiene
  • Day 1: do not brush the surgical sites. You may gently brush all other teeth as normal.
  • From Day 2: gently rinse with warm salt water after each meal — 1 teaspoon of salt dissolved in a glass of warm water. Let the water gently flow around your mouth and drain out; do not swish vigorously.
  • From Day 2–3: resume gentle brushing of all teeth, carefully avoiding the extraction sockets. A soft-bristled toothbrush is recommended.
  • Do not use commercial mouthwash (e.g. Listerine) in the first 48 hours — the alcohol content can irritate healing tissue.
What to Avoid
  • Smoking — significantly increases the risk of dry socket (painful failure of clot formation). Avoid for at least 5 days, ideally 2 weeks or longer.
  • Straws — the suction force dislodges the blood clot essential to healing.
  • Spitting forcefully — same reason as straws; avoid for the first 24–48 hours.
  • Touching or probing the socket with your tongue, fingers, or any object.
  • Strenuous exercise for 3–5 days — elevated heart rate and blood pressure increase bleeding risk.
  • Alcohol for 48 hours after surgery.
  • Flying within 48 hours if possible — air pressure changes can affect healing sockets.
Dry Socket — What to Watch For

Dry socket (alveolar osteitis) occurs when the blood clot in the extraction socket fails to form properly or is dislodged before healing is complete. It affects approximately 5–30% of lower wisdom tooth removals, and is significantly more common in smokers.

Signs of dry socket: a significant worsening of pain beginning 3–5 days after surgery (rather than improving), a bad taste or smell from the socket, and visible empty bone in the socket.

What to do: contact the clinic as soon as possible. Dry socket is treatable — we will place a soothing dressing in the socket and provide additional pain relief. It resolves with appropriate care.

When to Call Us / Seek Urgent Care

Call us on 1300 111 324 if you experience:

  • Significant worsening pain after day 3 — this may indicate dry socket or infection.
  • Fever above 38°C — may indicate infection.
  • Swelling that is increasing after day 3 rather than gradually improving.
  • Difficulty swallowing or opening your mouth that is worsening rather than improving.
Go to your nearest emergency department or call 000 if you experience:
  • Heavy, uncontrolled bleeding that does not respond to pressure.
  • Difficulty breathing or swallowing (could indicate spreading infection).
  • Severe allergic reaction to a prescribed medication (rash, hives, swollen throat, difficulty breathing).
Your Follow-up Appointment

A follow-up appointment has been arranged for you. Please attend even if you are feeling well — this allows your surgeon to check healing and address any concerns.
