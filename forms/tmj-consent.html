About the Procedures

The temporomandibular joint (TMJ) connects your lower jaw to your skull. TMJ disorders can cause pain, clicking, locking, and restricted jaw movement. Your surgeon has recommended one of the following procedures based on your diagnosis and the findings of your clinical and imaging assessment.

TMJ Arthroscopy (keyhole surgery)
A small camera (arthroscope) and fine instruments are inserted into the joint through two tiny incisions. The joint can be washed out (lavage), adhesions released, and the disc repositioned where possible. Arthroscopy is a day procedure with a faster recovery than open surgery. It is used for earlier-stage joint disease or when conservative treatment has not been sufficient.
TMJ Open Joint Surgery (arthroplasty / disc repositioning)
A larger incision is made in front of the ear to provide direct access to the joint. This allows for disc repair, repositioning, or removal (discectomy) where the disc is severely damaged. Open surgery is used when arthroscopy is insufficient or when more extensive joint work is required. Recovery takes longer than arthroscopy.
Total Joint Replacement (TJR)
The TMJ is replaced with a custom or stock prosthetic device, which may include a fossa component and a condylar component. Total joint replacement is reserved for severe joint destruction, ankylosis (bony fusion), or cases where previous surgery has failed. It is a longer procedure requiring hospital admission and carries a longer recovery period. The prosthetic device is designed to last many years, though revision surgery may ultimately be required.
Botulinum Toxin (Botox) Injection
Botulinum toxin is injected into the masseter and/or temporalis muscles to reduce the force of jaw clenching and bruxism. This is a non-surgical, office-based procedure. Effects typically last 3–4 months and repeat treatment is required to maintain benefit. It may be used alone or in combination with other TMJ treatments.
Risks & Complications

No surgical procedure is without risk. Your surgeon has recommended this procedure because the expected benefits outweigh the risks. Please read the following carefully.

Very Common — most patients experience these
Jaw swelling, stiffness and discomfort — expected after all TMJ procedures; managed with pain relief, ice, and jaw physiotherapy.
Temporary worsening of symptoms — it is common for symptoms to be worse in the first few weeks before improvement is seen, as the joint settles after intervention.
Restricted mouth opening during recovery — jaw exercises and physiotherapy are often required to regain mouth opening.
Common — may affect 1 in 5 to 1 in 20 patients
Temporary altered sensation around the ear and jaw — due to proximity of the auriculotemporal nerve; usually resolves within weeks.
Scar formation at incision site — a scar in front of the ear is expected with open procedures; usually fades well but may occasionally be noticeable.
Incomplete resolution of symptoms — TMJ conditions are complex and multi-factorial. Surgery may significantly improve but not fully resolve pain, clicking, or limited opening.
Recurrence of symptoms over time — ongoing joint degeneration or parafunctional habits may lead to symptom recurrence after an initial period of improvement.
Uncommon — may affect 1 in 100 patients
Wound infection — requiring antibiotic treatment and, rarely, further surgical management.
Haematoma (blood collection) — accumulation of blood around the surgical site, which may require drainage.
Damage to parotid gland or duct — the parotid salivary gland is adjacent to the TMJ; injury may cause swelling or salivary fistula.
Hearing changes — temporary changes in hearing or ear fullness; usually transient.
Joint stiffness (trismus) — scar tissue formation may limit jaw opening and require prolonged physiotherapy.
Rare — fewer than 1 in 100 patients
Facial nerve injury — the facial nerve passes in close proximity to the TMJ surgical site. Injury may cause temporary or permanent weakness of facial muscles. See note below.
Vascular injury — injury to vessels in the region of the TMJ; requires urgent management.
Implant complications (TJR) — dislocation, fracture, wear debris, or infection of prosthetic components, potentially requiring revision surgery.
Total joint revision surgery — a small number of TJR patients require further surgery due to implant failure, infection, or unsatisfactory outcome.
Note regarding facial nerve injury: The facial nerve and its branches pass in close proximity to the TMJ surgical site. Temporary weakness of facial muscles (forehead, eye, or lower face) is uncommon; permanent facial nerve injury is rare (less than 1% of cases) but may result in facial asymmetry, drooping, or difficulty closing the eye. Your surgeon will take every precaution to identify and protect the facial nerve during surgery.
Alternatives to Surgery

Conservative management — occlusal splints, physiotherapy, anti-inflammatory medications, and behavioural modification (habit correction, stress management) are first-line treatments for most TMJ disorders.

Arthrocentesis — a simpler, minimally invasive procedure involving joint washout using needles and saline, performed under local anaesthetic. Less comprehensive than arthroscopy but carries lower risk.

Botulinum toxin injection — for muscle-related TMJ (bruxism and clenching), botulinum toxin injections offer a non-surgical option.

Ongoing monitoring without surgery — appropriate for some patients whose condition is stable or progressing slowly.

Surgery is recommended when conservative measures have failed to provide adequate relief, or when the degree of joint pathology warrants surgical intervention. Please discuss alternatives with your surgeon before signing this form.
