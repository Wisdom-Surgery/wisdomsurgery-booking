About the Procedure

A dental implant is a titanium fixture placed into the jawbone to replace the root of a missing tooth. Over a period of 3–6 months, the implant integrates with the surrounding bone in a process called osseointegration. Once integration is confirmed, a crown, bridge, or denture is attached to the implant by your restorative dentist.

Bone grafting or sinus lifting may be required if there is insufficient bone volume at the implant site. These procedures use bone substitute material (and sometimes your own bone) to rebuild bone before or at the time of implant placement.

The procedure is typically performed under local anaesthetic with or without sedation. You may experience swelling, bruising, and discomfort for 1–2 weeks following surgery.

Risks & Complications

No surgical procedure is without risk. Your surgeon has recommended this procedure because the expected benefits outweigh the risks. Please read the following carefully.

Very Common — most patients experience these
Swelling, bruising and discomfort — expected for 1–2 weeks following surgery. Managed with ice packs, rest, and pain relief.
Temporary sensitivity at the implant site — the gum and bone around the implant may be tender while healing.
Common — may affect 1 in 5 to 1 in 20 patients
Implant failure — failure to osseointegrate (integrate with bone) occurs in approximately 5% of implants overall. Rates are higher in smokers, people with uncontrolled diabetes, and those with insufficient bone. A failed implant can be removed and a new one placed after healing.
Minor bleeding at the surgical site — some oozing is normal and controlled with gauze pressure.
Uncommon — may affect 1 in 100 patients
Wound infection — may threaten implant integration and require antibiotic treatment or, rarely, implant removal.
Damage to adjacent teeth or existing restorations — instruments used during implant placement may inadvertently affect neighbouring teeth.
Bone graft failure — grafted bone may not integrate adequately, potentially compromising implant placement.
Implant malposition — if the implant is not in the ideal position, removal and replacement may be necessary.
Rare — fewer than 1 in 100 patients
Nerve injury — lower jaw implants placed near the inferior alveolar nerve may cause temporary or permanent numbness of the lower lip, chin, or tongue. Pre-operative imaging is used to avoid nerve proximity.
Sinus perforation — upper jaw implants near the maxillary sinus may communicate with the sinus; usually manageable with appropriate treatment.
Implant fracture — rare mechanical failure of the implant fixture itself.
Peri-implantitis — long-term bacterial infection around the implant leading to progressive bone loss; requires ongoing maintenance and oral hygiene to prevent. See note below.
Important — long-term implant success: Long-term implant success depends significantly on oral hygiene, non-smoking status, and regular dental maintenance. Smokers have 2–3 times the implant failure rate of non-smokers. Implants are not immune to disease — peri-implantitis (implant gum disease) affects an estimated 20% of implants over the long term without adequate maintenance. Regular professional cleaning and home oral hygiene are essential for the life of the implant.
Alternatives to Implants

Removable denture — a cost-effective option but may affect speech, eating, and comfort. Dentures do not prevent bone resorption at the tooth extraction site.

Fixed dental bridge — a fixed restoration supported by crowns on adjacent teeth. Does not require surgery, but requires grinding down of healthy adjacent teeth and does not preserve bone in the edentulous space.

No replacement — leaving the space unfilled can lead to drifting and tipping of adjacent teeth, over-eruption of the opposing tooth, and progressive bone loss at the site.

Implants are generally considered the gold standard for single tooth replacement due to their preservation of surrounding bone, natural function, and longevity. Please discuss these alternatives with your surgeon before signing this form.
