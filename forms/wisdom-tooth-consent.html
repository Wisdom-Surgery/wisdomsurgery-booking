Your Procedure — Please review before signing

About the Procedure

Wisdom tooth removal (third molar extraction) is a surgical procedure to remove one or more of the four permanent adult teeth at the back corners of the mouth. Wisdom teeth may need to be removed when they are impacted (unable to fully emerge), causing pain or infection, damaging adjacent teeth, or at risk of future problems.

The procedure involves numbing the area, making a small incision in the gum if necessary, removing any bone obstructing access to the tooth root, dividing the tooth if needed, and removing it in sections. The socket is then cleaned and sutured. Recovery typically takes 1–2 weeks.

Risks & Complications

No surgical procedure is without risk. Your surgeon has recommended this procedure because the expected benefits outweigh the risks. Please read the following carefully.

Very Common — most patients experience these
Swelling, bruising and discomfort — expected for 1–2 weeks following surgery. Managed with ice packs, rest, and pain relief.
Restricted jaw opening (trismus) — difficulty opening the mouth fully, usually resolving within 2–4 weeks.
Temporary bleeding — some oozing is normal for the first few hours and controlled by biting on gauze.
Common — may affect 1 in 5 to 1 in 20 patients
Dry socket (alveolar osteitis) — the blood clot in the socket fails to form or is dislodged, causing significant pain 3–5 days post-surgery. More common in smokers and those taking the oral contraceptive pill. Treated with dressings and pain relief.
Temporary altered sensation — numbness, tingling or altered sensation of the lower lip, chin or tongue may occur due to proximity of tooth roots to the inferior alveolar or lingual nerve. Typically resolves within days to weeks.
Uncommon — may affect 1 in 100 patients
Wound infection — requiring a course of antibiotics. Signs include worsening pain, swelling, or fever after day 3.
Damage to adjacent tooth or restoration — the tooth or filling next to the wisdom tooth may be inadvertently affected during removal.
Retained root fragment — occasionally a small root tip may be left in place if removal poses greater risk than benefit. Your surgeon will advise you if this occurs.
Rare — fewer than 1 in 100 patients
Permanent nerve injury — lasting numbness, tingling, or altered taste affecting the lip, chin, or tongue (inferior alveolar or lingual nerve). See note below.
Sinus communication (upper wisdom teeth only) — an opening between the tooth socket and the maxillary sinus. Small communications usually heal on their own; larger ones may require further treatment.
Jaw fracture — extremely rare; associated with deeply impacted lower wisdom teeth, particularly in older patients with dense bone.
Need for further surgery — a small number of patients require a further procedure to manage complications or incomplete removal.
Note regarding nerve injury: The inferior alveolar nerve (supplying sensation to the lower lip and chin) and the lingual nerve (supplying sensation to the tongue) run in close proximity to the roots of lower wisdom teeth. A temporary change in sensation is relatively common and usually resolves within days to weeks. Prolonged altered sensation beyond 6 months is uncommon. Permanent nerve injury is rare (estimated at less than 1%) but is a recognised risk, particularly with deeper impactions. Your surgeon will have assessed your X-rays to discuss your individual risk level.
Alternatives to Surgery

Your surgeon has recommended removal after assessing your clinical situation. The main alternative is conservative management — monitoring your wisdom teeth with regular clinical review and X-rays, without removing them.

However, leaving symptomatic or problematic wisdom teeth in place may result in: recurrent infection (pericoronitis), development of a cyst or tumour around the tooth, progressive damage to the adjacent second molar, increasing difficulty of removal as bone density increases with age, or ongoing pain and crowding.

If you would like to discuss alternatives further, please ask your surgeon before signing this form.
