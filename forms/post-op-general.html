Post-operative Instructions

General surgical recovery — please read carefully before leaving the clinic

1 What to Expect

Recovery after oral and maxillofacial surgery varies depending on the procedure performed. Some degree of discomfort, swelling, and fatigue is expected and normal in the first few days.

Day 1–2
Peak swelling and discomfort. Rest is important. You may feel drowsy from anaesthesia or sedation. Keep the area clean and follow wound care instructions.
Day 3–5
Swelling may continue or increase slightly before improving. Pain should be manageable with prescribed medication. Begin gentle mobilisation as directed.
Day 5–14
Gradual improvement. Sutures may dissolve or be removed at your follow-up appointment. Most patients return to light daily activity during this period.
Weeks 2–6
Continued healing. More extensive procedures (e.g. orthognathic surgery) may involve a longer recovery. Follow your surgeon's specific instructions for your procedure.
Important Everyone heals differently. If your recovery feels significantly different from what is described here, contact the clinic.

2 Pain Management & Medications

Take all prescribed medications as directed, even if you feel well. Do not skip doses in the early recovery period.

General guidance
  • Take pain relief before anaesthesia fully wears off — do not wait until severe pain sets in
  • Paracetamol and ibuprofen (if not contraindicated) can be alternated for better pain control
  • Antibiotics must be completed in full even if symptoms resolve earlier
  • If prescribed a stronger analgesic (e.g. oxycodone), take only as directed — do not drive or operate machinery
  • Anti-nausea medication may be prescribed — take this with food if possible
Blood thinners & anti-inflammatories Avoid aspirin and blood-thinning supplements (fish oil, vitamin E, turmeric) unless specifically prescribed. If you take prescribed anticoagulants, do not stop without speaking to your surgeon.

3 Wound & Surgical Site Care

Proper wound care is essential to prevent infection and support healing.

Intraoral wounds (inside the mouth)
  • Rinse gently with warm salt water (¼ teaspoon salt in a glass of warm water) starting 24 hours after surgery, 3–4 times daily
  • Do not rinse forcefully — gentle swishing only
  • Chlorhexidine (Savacol) mouthwash may be prescribed — use as directed, not in place of salt water
  • Avoid touching the wound with your fingers or tongue
  • Dissolvable sutures will disappear on their own within 1–2 weeks
  • Non-dissolvable sutures will be removed at your follow-up appointment
Extraoral wounds (on the face or neck)
  • Keep dressings clean and dry unless instructed otherwise
  • Dressings may be changed at home using the materials provided — ask staff if unsure
  • Do not apply creams, ointments, or makeup to wounds unless specifically prescribed
  • Protect healing scars from sun exposure for at least 6 months (SPF50+) to minimise scarring
  • Report any signs of wound breakdown, increasing redness, or discharge
Signs of infection Increasing redness, warmth, swelling beyond day 3, pus or unusual discharge, fever above 38°C, or a wound that appears to be opening — contact the clinic promptly.

4 Swelling & Bruising

Swelling and bruising are a normal part of the healing process and usually peak around 48–72 hours after surgery.

  • Apply an ice pack (wrapped in a cloth) to the outside of your face for 20 minutes on, 20 minutes off during the first 24 hours — do not apply ice directly to skin
  • After 48 hours, gentle warmth (warm face cloth) may help resolve bruising — do not apply heat in the first 24 hours
  • Sleep with your head elevated on 2 pillows to reduce swelling
  • Bruising may travel downward (e.g. into the neck or chest) — this is normal
  • Significant swelling usually resolves within 1–2 weeks; subtle swelling may persist for several weeks
Numbness or altered sensation Some procedures temporarily affect the nerves supplying the lip, chin, cheek, or tongue. This usually resolves over weeks to months. If you have concerns, discuss with your surgeon at follow-up.

5 Diet & Fluids

Good nutrition supports healing. Your diet may need to be modified depending on the procedure performed.

General dietary guidance
  • Start with clear fluids (water, diluted juice, broth) when you feel ready — usually 1–2 hours after returning home
  • Progress to soft foods (yoghurt, scrambled eggs, mashed potato, soup) as tolerated
  • Avoid hard, crunchy, or chewy foods until cleared by your surgeon
  • Avoid very hot foods and drinks in the first 24 hours
  • Stay well hydrated — aim for 6–8 glasses of water daily
  • Do not use a straw for at least 5–7 days (negative pressure can disrupt healing)
Jaw surgery or wiring If your jaw is wired or banded, you have specific dietary restrictions and emergency wire-cutting instructions. Please refer to your separate jaw surgery post-operative instructions.

6 Activity & Rest

Adequate rest in the first 48 hours is critical for recovery. Overdoing activity too early can increase bleeding, swelling, and pain.

First 24–48 hours
Rest at home. No strenuous physical activity. A responsible adult must remain with you if you had general anaesthesia or sedation.
Day 3–7
Light walking is encouraged. Avoid any activity that elevates your heart rate or blood pressure significantly.
Week 2 onwards
Gradually return to normal activity as tolerated. Exercise and return to work timelines vary — ask your surgeon at follow-up.
✗Gym, sport, heavy lifting
✗Swimming or water sports
✗Driving (first 24–48 hrs after GA)
✗Bending or heavy housework
✗Alcohol or recreational drugs
✗Smoking (delays healing)
Driving & legal decisions Do not drive, operate machinery, consume alcohol, or make important legal or financial decisions for at least 24 hours after general anaesthesia or sedation.

7 Oral Hygiene

Maintaining good oral hygiene reduces the risk of infection and supports healing, even when it feels uncomfortable.

  • Continue brushing teeth gently, avoiding the surgical site for the first few days
  • Use a soft-bristled toothbrush and be especially gentle near wounds
  • Salt water rinses (starting 24 hours post-op) are important — do not skip these
  • Avoid electric toothbrushes near wound sites until healed
  • If you have orthodontic brackets or splints, clean around them carefully as directed

8 When to Call Us or Seek Emergency Care

Go to the Emergency Department immediately if you experience:
  • Difficulty breathing or swallowing that is worsening
  • Rapidly spreading swelling of the face, floor of mouth, or neck
  • Uncontrolled or severe bleeding that does not stop with pressure
  • Chest pain, shortness of breath, or palpitations
  • Sudden severe headache, vision changes, or weakness
  • High fever with rigors (uncontrolled shivering)
  • Loss of consciousness or seizures

Contact our clinic during business hours if you experience:

  • Increasing pain after day 3 that is not controlled by prescribed analgesia
  • Swelling that continues to increase beyond day 4
  • Fever above 38°C that does not settle
  • Signs of infection (pus, foul odour, increasing redness)
  • Wound breakdown or sutures coming out prematurely
  • Numbness that seems to be spreading or worsening
  • Any concern about your recovery that is worrying you
