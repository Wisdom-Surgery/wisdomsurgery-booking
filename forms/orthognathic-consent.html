Anaesthesia

Orthognathic surgery is performed under general anaesthesia in hospital. A separate consent will be required from your anaesthetist.

About the Procedure

Orthognathic surgery repositions the upper jaw (maxilla), lower jaw (mandible), or both to correct significant skeletal discrepancies that cannot be adequately addressed by orthodontic treatment alone. The surgery is performed under general anaesthesia in hospital.

The bones are carefully cut and repositioned according to pre-operative surgical planning, then fixed in their new position with titanium plates and screws. Recovery involves a soft or liquid diet for 4–8 weeks while the jaw heals. Swelling is significant and expected.

Orthognathic surgery is typically part of a combined orthodontic-surgical treatment plan. Orthodontic treatment is required both before surgery (to align the teeth for the new jaw position) and after surgery (for fine-tuning the bite). The full treatment course typically spans 18–36 months.

Risks & Complications

No surgical procedure is without risk. Your surgeon has recommended this procedure because the expected benefits outweigh the risks. Please read the following carefully.

Very Common — most patients experience these
Swelling and bruising — significant, peaking at 48–72 hours and persisting for weeks to months. This is expected and is part of normal healing.
Temporary numbness or altered sensation — affecting the face, lips, chin and teeth. Sensory nerves are stretched during surgery and altered sensation is expected during recovery.
Restricted mouth opening and jaw stiffness — due to post-operative swelling and muscle guarding. Physiotherapy may be required.
Liquid/soft food diet for 4–8 weeks — while the jaw bones heal and fixation plates stabilise the new position.
Temporary changes to speech and nasal airway — particularly following Le Fort I surgery; usually resolves as swelling subsides.
Common — may affect 1 in 5 to 1 in 20 patients
Temporary altered bite sensation and tooth sensitivity — the bite may feel different as the jaw settles; teeth may be temporarily sensitive.
Partial relapse — some movement of the jaws back toward their original position is common (10–30%) and may be managed with orthodontics or, in significant cases, further surgery.
Sinus congestion and altered nasal breathing — particularly following Le Fort I (upper jaw) surgery; generally resolves over weeks to months.
Headaches — common in the post-operative period, typically managed with pain relief.
Uncommon — may affect 1 in 100 patients
Wound infection — requiring antibiotics and, rarely, surgical drainage.
Hardware discomfort — titanium plates and screws rarely cause irritation and may occasionally need to be removed after healing is complete.
Significant relapse requiring further surgery — in a minority of patients, relapse is sufficient to require surgical correction.
Unsatisfactory aesthetic result — the final result may not exactly match surgical planning due to healing variation, swelling, or soft tissue response.
TMJ changes — temporomandibular joint symptoms may improve, remain unchanged, or occasionally worsen following jaw surgery.
Rare — fewer than 1 in 100 patients
Permanent nerve injury — lasting numbness or altered sensation of the face, lip, or chin (inferior alveolar, mental, or infraorbital nerves). See note below.
Non-union — failure of the bone to heal correctly, requiring further intervention such as revision fixation or bone grafting.
Avascular necrosis — loss of blood supply to a bone segment, potentially requiring further surgery.
Airway complications — due to swelling or anaesthesia; managed in a hospital setting.
Vascular injury — injury to major blood vessels, requiring urgent intervention.
Vision disturbance — extremely rare following Le Fort I surgery; related to orbital/retrobulbar complications.
Note regarding sensory nerve changes: Sensory nerve changes are very common after orthognathic surgery due to the proximity of the inferior alveolar, mental, and infraorbital nerves to the surgical sites. Temporary numbness or altered sensation affecting the lips, chin, cheeks, and upper teeth is expected and usually resolves over weeks to months. Permanent nerve injury is rare but recognised, and patients should be aware of this risk prior to consenting.
Alternatives to Surgery

Conservative management with orthodontics alone — limited by the degree of skeletal discrepancy. Orthodontic treatment can move teeth but cannot reposition the underlying jaw bones.

Camouflage orthodontics — teeth are moved to compensate for the skeletal problem. This approach may compromise long-term stability, periodontal health, or facial aesthetics, and is unsuitable for significant skeletal discrepancies.

Waiting or deferral — surgery does not become easier with age. Deferring treatment may increase surgical complexity as bone density increases and growth is completed.

Surgery is recommended when the skeletal discrepancy cannot be adequately addressed by orthodontics alone, or where the functional and aesthetic outcomes of surgery are significantly superior to non-surgical alternatives. Please discuss alternatives with your surgeon before signing this form.
